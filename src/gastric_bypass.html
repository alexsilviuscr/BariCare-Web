Gastric bypass

The gastric bypass, also known as the 'Roux-en-Y,' derives its name from the French term meaning 'in the form of the letter Y.' This procedure shapes your small intestine accordingly.

Initially, surgeons create a small pouch at the top of the stomach using surgical staples, separating it from the lower portion. Following this, they divide the small intestine and connect the new segment to the stomach pouch.

Food now flows through the smaller stomach and lower small intestine, bypassing the rest. This dual restriction limits stomach capacity and nutrient absorption, enhancing effectiveness compared to gastric restriction alone.

Advantages

01

Reliable and long-lasting weight loss

02

Effective for remission of obesity-associated conditions

03

Refined and standardized technique

Disadvantages

01

More vitamin and mineral deficiencies than sleeve gastrectomy or gastric banding

02

There is a risk for small bowel complications and obstruction

03

May cause “dumping syndrome”, a feeling of sickness after eating or drinking, especially sweets
